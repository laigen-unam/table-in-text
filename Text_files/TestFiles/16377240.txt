Abstract
Although the avrA gene is prevalent among 80 % of the Salmonella enterica serovars , only a small number of them usually express the respective virulence-associated effector protein AvrA . 
However , under culture conditions below pH 6.0 many of the AvrA non-producer strains ( e.g. S. Agona , S. Bovismorbiﬁcans , S. Virchow ) begin to produce AvrA , while some others remain silent . 
Four phenotypical classes of S. enterica were identiﬁed under deﬁned standard culture conditions : class 1 comprises strains with a constitutive synthesis of AvrA ; class 2 comprises strains with an acid induction of AvrA ; class 3 comprises strains with silent avrA genes ; and the fourth class comprises strains which do not contain the avrA gene ( class 0 strains ) . 
The expression of avrA was found to be controlled by a Salmonella-speciﬁc mechanism because cloned avrA genes from classes 1 , 2 , and 3 strains remain silent in Escherichia coli strains , while easily expressed in S. enterica strains . 
The expression of AvrA in classes 1 , 2 , and 3 strains does not coincide with the nucleotide sequences of the respective promoter or structural regions of the avrA genes , but depends directly on this Salmonella-speciﬁc regulatory system which appears to be differently modulated in the distinct Salmonella serovars .

Introduction
Salmonellosis is one of the most frequently identiﬁed food-borne infections worldwide ( Miller et al. , 1995 ) . 
Its aetiological agent , Salmonella enterica ( S. enterica ) , reveals a considerable biological diversity with respect to its genetically different , polymorphic serovars and clonal types ( Popoff , 2001 ; Beltran et al. , 1988 ; Selander et al. , 1990 ) . 
In contrast to this great biological diversity , only a small number of serovars have attracted clinical and epidemiological attention , e.g. S. enterica serovar Enteritidis ( S. Enteritidis ) or serovar Typhimurium ( S. Typhimurium ) . 
Strains belonging to these both serovars have been identiﬁed frequently among foodborne infections and epidemic outbreaks all over the countries of the northern hemisphere ( see Olsen et al. , 2001 ; Saeed , 1999 ) , while other Salmonella serotype such as S. Agona , S. Bovismorbiﬁcans , S. Hadar , S. Virchow , and others appeared very rarely and only exceptionally in epidemic outbreaks ( see Puohiniemi et al. , 1997 ; Rabsch et al. , 2005 ; Liesegang et al. , 2002 ; Todd and Murdoch , 1983 ) . 
The molecular mechanisms for this broad range of virulence and epidemic potency of the various Salmonella serovars are still unknown . 
There is a strong evidence that type III secretion systems ( TTSS ; Hueck , 1998 ) , which translocate an array of virulence-associated proteins ( effector proteins ) into the target cells ( Galan , 2001 ; Hensel , 2000 ) , are involved in the virulence of salmonellae . 
A large number of such translocated effector proteins affecting various cellular functions have been identiﬁed ( Wallis and Galyov , 2000 ; Galan , 2001 ; Hensel , 2000 ; Zhang et al. , 2003 ) . 
One of them is SopB , an inositol-4-phosphatase , which interferes with the inositol phosphate network controlling the inﬂux / efﬂux of water and electrolytes , IL8 production ( inﬂammation ) , as well as actin rearrangements ( Norris et al. , 1998 ; Steele-Mortimer et al. , 2000 ; Feng et al. , 2001 ; Gewirtz et al. , 2000 ; Eckmann et al. , 1997 ; Hobbie et al. , 1997 ) . 
The recently identiﬁed S. enterica effector protein AvrA also contributes to the complexity of the virulence network of salmonellae ( Collier-Hyams et al. , 2002 ) . 
AvrA is a cysteine protease , which helps to induce programmed cell death ( PCD ) and to inhibit IL8 production and in turn the inﬂammatory response of hosts against the infection . 
AvrA is related in structure , function , and cellular effects to similar proteins from plant pathogens ( Büttner and Bonas , 2002 ) and from Yersinia spp . 
( Mills et al. , 1997 ; Orth et al. , 1999 ) , however , affecting different steps of the PCD pathways ( Collier-Hyams et al. , 2002 ) . 
AvrA was earlier regarded as an anti-virulence protein ( Hardt and Galan , 1997 ) as it provokes a kind of `` scorched earth policy '' of the target organisms through preventing the colonisation and invasion of the pathogens ( Büttner and Bonas , 2002 ) . 
However , since AvrA can induce not only PCD of cellular immune response systems but also inhibits the IL8 and TNF-a production of target cells which will paralyse their inﬂammatory responses , the effector protein AvrA can also be regarded as an virulenceassociated protein . 
Most of the S. enterica strains ( 80 % ) contain the avrA gene , and only some serovars such as S. Typhi , and S. Paratyphi , which evade epithelial defences resulting in severe systemic disease ( within macrophages ) , do not possess it ( Hardt and Galan , 1997 ; Prager et al. , 2000 , 2003 ) . 
However , using recombinant protein technology and monovalent but polyclonal antibodies in order to identify the expression of AvrA in different S. enterica strains , it turned out that most of the avrA-positive S. enterica serovars did not express AvrA ( Streckel et al. , 2004 ) . 
Surprisingly , only among epidemic strains of S. Typhimurium and S. Enteritidis , AvrA was detected together with SopB , SopD1 , and SopE1 in the supernatants . 
In order to study avrA regulation and identify the reason ( s ) for lack of avrA expression among most of the various Salmonella serovars and strains ( in contrast to other effector proteins such as SopB ) , AvrA nonproducer strains of S. enterica were submitted to various culture conditions . 
Only the pH 6.0 culture conditions were found to induce AvrA in some of the strains ( Streckel et al. , 2004 ) , while others remained negative . 
Such a diversity in the induction of AvrA could be either due to nucleotide sequence differences of the promoter region of avrA giving rise to binding differences of an acid-resistant/acid-sensitive repressor/activator protein , respectively , or to differences in a regulative system , e.g. the acid tolerance response ( ATR ) network of S. enterica ( Foster et al. , 1994 ) , which might be involved in the expression of AvrA . 
In this communication , data on expression and nucleotide sequencing of the effector protein gene avrA among S. enterica strains are summarised in order to begin disclosing the regulation of AvrA expression and its probable involvement in the virulence of S. enterica .

Plasmids
The plasmids pSTM310 and pSTM320 originated from PCR cloning of S. Typhimurium DT2 ( 04-419 ) DNA , the plasmids pSV210 and pSV220 , from 
S. Virchow ( SV23 ) DNA , and the plasmid pSB869 from SL1344 ( Table 2 ) . 
PCR cloning was carried out using oligonucleotides designed to ﬂank the promoter and gene sequence of avrA according to GenBank accession number gi16763390 . 
Oligonucleotide primer were avrA_pr-F ( 50-cagctcgagtggtctgggaacaaaaaccg-30 ) and avrA_ge-R ( 50-cgcaagcttggatcccggtttaagtaaagactta-ta-30 ) . 
The respective ampliﬁed product consisting of the native sequence without the stop codon ( 1256 bp ) , was cloned into pcDNA 3.1myc-His A ( ) ( Invitrogen ) via BamHI-XhoI giving rise to the plasmids pSV220 and pSTM320 , respectively . 
The insertion was conﬁrmed by PCR and sequencing . 
The reporter plasmids pSV210 and pSTM310 were constructed by subcloning the avrA promoters of the respective strains into the BamHI-XhoI sites upstream of a promoter-less green ﬂuorescent protein ( GFP ) - encoding sequence on the low-copy-number vector pCS21 ( Kalir et al. , 2001 ) . 
The promoter region of the respective salmonellae was ampliﬁed by PCR using primers designed to ﬂank the promoter sequence ( GenBank accession number gi16763390 ) together with 125 bp of the proximal part of the avrA structural gene . 
Oligonucleotide primers were avrA_pr-F ( see above ) and avrA_pr-R ( 50-catggatccaatgcttcggtgtttagctccc-30 ) . 
The ampliﬁed products retained the native sequence including the native start codon , ribosome-binding sites , promoter elements , and 125 bp from avrA that may contain an additional control element . 
The total length of the cloned sequence is 475 bp . 
Between the reading frame and the GFP-encoding sequence , a strong ribosome-binding site is located to strengthen the signal . 
The insertion was conﬁrmed by PCR and sequencing . 
For negative control , PCR fragments were also cloned in the opposite direction into the XhoI-BamHI sites of pCS21b giving rise to the plasmids pSV210r and pSTM310r ( see footnote c , Table 2 ) plasmids . 
Plasmids were introduced into the respective S. enterica and E. coli strains by electroporation ( Ausubel et al. , 1997 ) .

Culture conditions for the production of effector proteins
One colony of each S. enterica strain was resuspended in 5 ml LB +0.3 M NaCl and incubated for 6 h at 37 1C ( OD of 0.7 -- 0.8 ) under shaking on a longitudinal shaker ( 100 rpm ) . 
Five ml of this pre-culture were diluted into 15 ml fresh warm LB +0.3 M NaCl using a 100-ml bulb ﬂask with narrow neck and incubated overnight on a longitudinal shaker ( 100 rpm ) . 
Variations of this standard culture conditions referred to temperature ( 30 1C instead of 37 1C ) , to pH 6.0 and 8.0 instead of pH 7.4 , to iron deﬁciency ( according to Reissbrodt et al. ( 1996 ) using ethylenediamine-N , N0-bis ( 2 hydroxyphenylacetic acid ) ) and to addition of 40 mM CaCl2 , 40 mM MgSO4 , as well as freeze -- thaw cell lysates of 10 HeLa cells pro 7 ml , respectively . 
These variations were carried out in the pre-culture as well as in the main culture ( except for iron deﬁciency and cell lysates ) . 
Concerning the pH variation of the standard culture conditions , the applied pH values remain stable over the logarithmic phase of growth ( 4 -- 5 h ) but changed to pH 7.9 -- 8.4 in the early stationary phase of growth , irrespective of the initial pH values . 
This implies that the tested pH values are only present in the logarithmic phase of growth . 
Expression studies of avrA 
Production and harvesting of AvrA and SopB proteins
The effector proteins SopB and AvrA were puriﬁed from whole cells and from the supernatants of the respective Salmonella cultures . 
Cultures were transferred to an ice bath for 30 min . 
The supernatants of the respective 20-ml cultures ( see above ) were harvested by centrifugation ( 1 h , 20,000 rpm ) and ﬁltration through Millipore ﬁlters ( 0.45 mm ) . 
Precipitation of the proteins from the supernatants was carried out using 10 % trichloroacetic acid on ice for 1 h . 
After 1 h centrifugation at 20,000 rpm the respective precipitates were transferred to 0.4 ml 0.1 M NaOH and 2.0 ml ice-cold acetone ( 20 1C ) . 
After 20 min at 20 1C precipitates were harvested by centrifugation for 20 min at 20,000 rpm , washed again with ice-cold acetone , and harvested by centrifugation . 
The sediments were dried overnight , and then dissolved in 100 ml Laemmli buffer , heated for 5 min at 95 1C and subsequently submitted to SDS-PAGE . 
In order to identify AvrA and SopB bound to cellular compartments , the bacterial sediments described above were resuspended 1:100 in Laemmli buffer , heated for 5 min at 95 1C and subsequently submitted to SDS-PAGE . 
The cell pellets were tested for the presence of AvrA only when AvrA was absent in the respective supernatants .

Results
Phenotypic characterisation and classiﬁcation of the avrA gene expression 
In contrast to SopB , which is produced by nearly all S. enterica strains and , therefore , was used as a positive internal control for expression and translocation of effector proteins under standard culture conditions ( Fig. 2 , Table 3 ) , AvrA appeared to be differently expressed . 
Four different classes of S. enterica strains have been established ( Table 3 ) : class 1 S. enterica strains express AvrA constitutively ( e.g. S. Typhimur-ium DT104 no. 97-01208 ; S. Enteritidis PT4 , no. 04-0762 ) ; class 2 strains express AvrA only under acidic culture conditions ( e.g. S. Paratyphi B-EPV-1 , no. 00-1205 ; S. Bovismorbiﬁcans , no. 97-12539 ) ; class 3 strains do not express AvrA under all conditions tested as far ( see Material and methods ) ( e.g. S. Derby , no. 02-03424 ; S. Typhimurium DT204 , no. 00-2379 ) ; and class 0 
S. enterica strains ( S. Paratyphi B-SPV , 99-9330 , S. Paratyphi B-EPV-2 , 00-1035 ) do not harbour the avrA gene ( Table 3 ) . 
All tested S. enterica strains belonging to different serotypes were investigated for the presence of AvrA in supernatants ( translocation ) as well as bound in the cytoplasm ( translation ) ( Table 3 ) . 
Most of the AvrA-negative strains ( as listed in Table 3 ) could be deﬁned as negatives not only by the absence of the AvrA in supernatants but also in the cytoplasm . 
Although a very small amount of AvrA ( 0.5 mg/109 cells ) could be identiﬁed in a few strains ( Table 3 ) , it is suggested that AvrA is not produced in the negative strains . 
In order to analyse the molecular mechanisms of AvrA expression , the nucleotide sequences of the corresponding avrA genes from the AvrA classes 1 , 2 , and 3 were compared . 
The cloned avrA genes were also tested for their transcription , translation/translocation in different S. enterica hosts belonging to the four above mentioned classes as well as in E. coli K12 . 
Comparative nucleotide sequence analysis of avrA genes from S. enterica strains belonging to the different AvrA expression classes
In contrast to the sopB genes which reveal a distinct polymorphism in various S. enterica strains ( Prager et al. , 2000 ) , the avrA genes ( Fig. 3A ) and the upstream promoters ( Fig. 3B ) sequenced from S. Typhimurium DT2 and S. Enteritidis PT11 ( class 1 ) , S. Virchow 97-4315 and S. Paratyphi B-EPV-1 00-1205 ( class 2 ) , as well as S. Derby 02-3424 and S. Typhimurium SL1344 ( class 3 ) appeared rather conserved ( Fig. 3 ) . 
The few nucleo-tide sequence variations ( Fig. 3 ) do not correlate with the expression phenotypes of the three AvrA classes ( Table 3 ) . 
Therefore , we conclude that probably other systems might be responsible for the regulation of AvrA expression in S. enterica strains . 
Expression studies of avrA genes using GFP as a marker
Expression studies of avrA from the various S. enterica strains belonging to the above mentioned AvrA classes ( Table 3 ) were carried out using a fusion of the gfp gene to the avrA promoter . 
The plasmids pSTM310 and pSV210 ( Table 2 ) carrying the respective promoter and 125 nucleotides of the avrA structural gene proximal to the gfp gene were tested in E. coli K12 ( DH5a , XL-1 blue ) as well as in S. enterica strains ( Table 4 ) . 
No GFP signal could be detected with all plasmids in E. coli K12 ( Table 4 , Fig. 4 ) grown under various culture conditions ( see Materials and methods ) as well as with the plasmids pSV210r and pSTM310r ( see Table 2 ) serving as the negative control in E. coli and Salmonella strains . 
In contrast , high intensity o ﬂuorescence was detected with all avrA constructs in all Salmonella strains belonging to the three AvrA expression classes ( Table 4 , Fig. 4 ) , even in class 3 strains . 
The ﬂuorescence signals obtained with the avrA plasmids were 2 - to 3-fold higher than those obtained with the rob promoter ( positive control ) , and the expression levels were very similar in all the applied Salmonella strains irrespective of the origin of the avrA gene ( Table 4 ) . 
In all Salmonella strains the highest rate of expression was observed in mid-logarithmic phase of growth and then declined toward the stationary phase ( Fig. 5 ) . 
Fluorescence was also measured during growth under acidic conditions ( pH 6.0 ) . 
Although ﬂuorescence was slightly higher under acidic conditions for some of the Salmo-nella strains ( Table 4 , Fig. 4 ) , these differences were not statistically signiﬁcant ( P 1/4 0:05 ) .

As expected , no AvrA proteins could be found in supernatants or bound to cells in the plasmid-carrying E. coli K12 ( DH5a , XL-1blue ) . 
In contrast , the respective plasmids allow a considerable translation as well as translocation of AvrA in the tested S. enterica strains ( Table 4 ) . 
However , different expression patterns were observed : pSV210 , pSV220 , and pSB869 led to constitutive AvrA expression in all S. enterica strains ( with the exception that pSV220 in class 2 strains remain acid-inducible ) , whereas the plasmids pSTM310 and pSTM320 led to AvrA expression only in class 0 and class 1 strains ( Table 4 ) . 
Therefore , we suggest that a Salmonella-speciﬁc regulatory system is required to conduct the expression of AvrA . 
This regulatory network appears to be modulated in various S. enterica strains in response to different environmental signals .

Discussion
Virulence factors can be co-ordinately regulated by a variety of environmental signals such as temperature , osmolarity and anaerobiosis as well as pH ( Miller et al. , 1989 ) . 
Since during infections bacteria are exposed to low pH , e.g. in the stomach , or within the phagosomes of intestinal epithelial cells or macrophages , it appeared reasonable that low pH can function to induce several virulence factors ( Foster et al. , 1994 ; Foster , 1995 ) . 
When testing different environmental signals ( e.g. the presence of eukaryotic cell components , various concentrations of Fe , Mg , and Ca , respectively , 3 + 2 + 2 + variations in temperature and pH ) , Streckel et al. ( 2004 ) observed that culture conditions below pH 6.0 allowed the production of the effector proteins AvrA and SopD by some of the tested S. enterica strains , while they remained negative at pH 7.4 as well as under other culture conditions . 
In this communication the expression of AvrA in different S. enterica strains was studied under pH 7.4 and 6.0 culture conditions as well as in E. coli harbouring the respective AvrA-encoding plasmids . 
As shown in Table 4 and Fig. 4 the plasmid-localised avrA genes were transcribed ( GFP signals ) in all Salmonella strains tested but no expression in E. coli K12 strains was found , irrespective of the culture conditions . 
The highest rates of expression by salmonellae were found during logarithmic growth ( Fig. 5 ) . 
Western blot analysis revealed also the translation of plasmid-encoded AvrA in all S. enterica but not in E. coli strains ( Table 4 , but see the exception of pSTM310 and pSV220 in class 0 strains ) . 
Both results point to a speciﬁc S. enterica background as a prerequisite for avrA expression . 
However , results for expression from AvrA-encoding plasmids in the various S. enterica strains are in contrast to results of Western blot analysis of AvrA synthesis in wild-type strains ( Table 3 ) . 
These S. enterica wild-type strains have been grouped into three expression classes : class 1 strains with constitutive expression of the avrA gene , class 2 strains with acid-inducible AvrA synthesis , and class 3 strains without any AvrA protein production . 
Since these differences in the expression of avrA among the classes 1 , 2 , and 3 strains are not due to differences in their promoter o structural gene sequences ( Fig. 3 ) and since the cloned avrA genes remain silent in E. coli K12 in contrast to S. enterica strains , a Salmonella-speciﬁc system is suggested which regulates the production of the effector protein AvrA . 
This as yet unknown regulative network must function in a kind of strain-speciﬁc manner : in some strains constitutively ( class 1 strains ) , in some others acid-inducible ( class 2 strains ) , or in some others in an unknown manner as yet ( class 3 strains ) . 
Why this proposed regulative system is not affecting the plasmidlocalised avrA genes remains to be shown . 
However , it is interesting to note that the plasmids pSTM310 and pSV220 in class 0 strains revealed an acid-inducible AvrA expression ( Table 4 ) . 
In contrast to AvrA and other virulence-associated proteins ( e.g. SopD , Streckel et al. , 2004 ) , the virulenceassociated effector protein SopB is independent of acid induction . 
Therefore , it is very unlikely that the TTSS itself is subjected to this as yet unknown Salmonella-speciﬁc regulative network ( compare Beuzon et al. , 1999 ; Rappl et al. , 2003 ) . 
Due to the acid induction of AvrA in class 2 strains , it might be speculated that the expression of avrA is dependent on a regulatory system which resembles the earlier identiﬁed ATR regulon , a part of a global stress response network including the functions of RpoS , Fur , and PhoP/Q ( Foster et al. , 1994 ; Foster , 1995 ) . 
Preliminary results using ATR mutant strains ( data to be published ) support this assumption . 
AvrA was earlier discussed to be an avirulence protein since S. Typhi and other systemic salmonellae do not contain the gene ( Hardt and Galan , 1997 ; CollierHyams et al. , 2002 ; Prager et al. , 2000 , 2003 ; Table 1 ) . 
However , Streckel et al. ( 2004 ) identiﬁed AvrA proteins frequently and in a great amount among virulent strains of S. enterica serovars Typhimurium ( e.g. the epidemic DT104 strain ) and Enteritidis ( e.g. the epidemic PT4 strain ) but only very rarely among other S. enterica serotypes . 
Both S. Enteritidis and S. Typhimurium serotypes are considered to be the most important aetiological agents of salmonellosis in northern countries while the other serotypes , e.g. S. Agona , S. Bovismorbiﬁcans , S. Hadar , S. Virchow ( Table 1 ) belong to less frequently observed salmonellae in clinic and epidemics ( however , with the exception of S. Virchow PT31 in Israel ) . 
Although AvrA , a cysteine protease , interferes with the apoptosis pathways of the target cells ( CollierHyams et al. , 2002 ) , it must be also regarded as an effector protein involved in the enteritis pathways . 
The action of AvrA leads to a local apoptosis ( scorched earth principle ) and a stop of inﬂammation ( IL8 ) , thereby inhibiting the invasion pathways of salmonello-sis but allowing and directing the enteritis pathways . 
Since in some S. enterica strains the AvrA protein has been identiﬁed as an acid-inducible protein ( Table 3 ) , these pathogens might express their full virulence phenotype only under acidic environmental conditions . 
This ought to be of relevance for food hygiene since several foods which have been found to be particularly risky for infections with salmonellae ( e.g. Leyer and Johnson , 1992 ) , are acidic foods ( e.g. mayonnaises , cheese , and products of it ) . 
It was discussed earlier that acidic conditions will induce an ATR and subsequently an acid resistance of salmonellae for a better surviving in the acidic environment of the stomach during the passages to the gut or afterwards into macrophages ( Foster , 1995 ) . 
The results summarised here support the view that also some of the virulence-associated proteins will be better produced in an acidic environment which in turns means that the virulence of the respective strains is enhanced under these conditions .
